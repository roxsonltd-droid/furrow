# The U.S. vs. Canada in May 2026: Trade War, Political Realignment, and a Wheat Earthquake — With Europe Caught in the Middle

*Analysis as of 19 May 2026*

## 1. The Current Political State — a "Rupture," Not a Dispute

Relations between Washington and Ottawa are now at their **lowest point in decades**, and this is no longer a diplomatic cliché but a structural description. What unfolded between March 2025 and May 2026 can be summed up in several key moments.

Trump imposed a series of tariffs — initially 25% under IEEPA (the International Emergency Economic Powers Act), then raised to 35% in August 2025. In February 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that IEEPA does not give the President authority to impose tariffs. The administration subsequently ended the IEEPA tariff actions and imposed a 10% "temporary import surcharge" for 150 days on most U.S. imports, including from Canada, under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974.

**But Section 232 (steel, aluminum, autos) remained** — and this is the real blow: in 2025, President Trump eliminated nearly all exemptions, including for Canada, from Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs (currently 50%). He also imposed tariffs on key Canadian sectors, including certain copper products, certain lumber and timber products, and vehicles and auto parts.

**Carney is not yielding to ultimatums.** Jamieson Greer (the U.S. Trade Representative) said the U.S. is unlikely to rubber-stamp the deal in July, hinting that longer negotiations lie ahead. When Carney's Canada-U.S. Economic Advisory Council met for the first time, the federal government made clear it has no intention of making concessions before broader CUSMA renewal talks begin.

## 2. Where the Two Countries Are Heading — in Diametrically Opposite Directions

### United States — "Fortress North America"

Trump has shaped a new doctrine in which "Fortress North America" is not a creative rebranding exercise. It is what remains available after the old vocabulary has been made dangerous to use for any company or industry group with business before the administration. The goal: keeping Canada and Mexico as exclusive trading partners, **bound to American economic security priorities**, particularly against China.

In April, U.S. Trade Representative Jamieson Greer told Congress that Canada is "doubling down on globalization," while the United States is trying to correct for the failures the administration feels globalization has caused. He also said the two approaches "don't fit together very well."

### Canada — Diversification and "Correcting the Weaknesses"

Carney has charted a fundamentally different course. As he put it: "The U.S. has fundamentally changed its approach to trade, raising its tariffs to levels last seen during the Great Depression. Many of our former strengths, based on our close ties to America, have become our weaknesses, weaknesses that we must correct."

The concrete steps:

- **New trade agreements with Europe, the United Kingdom, Asia, and the Pacific** — Carney's strategy is built on diversification.
- **Reorientation of oil exports**: in April 2026, Canada exported more seaborne crude oil to China than to the United States — for the first time, according to Reuters.
- **Boycotts**: Nova Scotia Liquor Corporation removed all alcohol from the United States from its shelves; Ontario's LCBO removed all U.S. alcohol from its shelves.

### The Paradox

Canada is condemned by geography to be dependent on the U.S. market. It cannot fully pivot away from the United States, but U.S.-Canada trade and tourism are falling, to say nothing of goodwill. In other words: Carney is moving toward diversification, but physical geography gives him only a limited window.

## 3. The USMCA Review on 1 July 2026 — the Main Showdown

By 1 July 2026, the United States, Mexico, and Canada must agree to extend the USMCA for 16 years in its current form, or approve a revised agreement, or a ten-year annual-review sunset countdown begins, with the agreement ending on 1 July 2036.

**The chances of a clean renewal are zero.** Even if consensus isn't reached immediately, the agreement will likely remain in force, at least for some time. A transition period of serial annual reviews is expected, which creates enormous uncertainty for investors and supply chains.

There is also a nuance — the U.S. prefers **bilateral** negotiations (U.S.-Mexico and U.S.-Canada separately), rather than trilateral ones, which **effectively divides the North American bloc**. Washington has been meeting with Mexico's top economic officials while sidelining Canada.

## 4. Wheat Forecasts for 2026 — Where the Story Gets Dramatic

This is where it gets serious, because it arrives **at exactly the worst possible moment** — Hormuz is blocked, fertilizers are expensive, and now yields are falling.

### United States — a Sharp Decline

According to the **USDA WASDE report of 12 May 2026**:

U.S. wheat production for 2026/27 is projected at 1,561 million bushels, down 424 million from last year, with the all-wheat yield forecast at 47.5 bushels per acre — 5.8 bushels below last year's record — pushing the season-average farm price up $1.50 to $6.50 per bushel.

That is a **21%** year-on-year drop. More specifically:

USDA's National Agricultural Statistics Service forecasts winter wheat production to be the smallest since 1965/66, with reduced production for all winter classes. In addition to declining area, yields this year are down with a significant, widespread drought, which has particularly affected Hard Red Winter.

Total U.S. all-wheat production is forecast at the lowest level since 1972/73. This is not a small drop — it is **the smallest U.S. crop in more than half a century**.

The cause: **drought in Kansas, Oklahoma, and Texas** + reduced acreage due to low prices in previous years + fertilizer costs that just surged because of the Iran crisis.

### Canada — Also a Decline, but a Softer One

Canada's situation is different:

At the national level, farmers expect to plant 26.7 million acres of wheat in 2026, down 1.1% from the previous year. Producers expect spring wheat area to edge down 0.1% to 18.8 million acres in 2026. Durum wheat area is anticipated to decrease 2.4% to 6.4 million acres, while winter wheat area is expected to fall 6.7% to 1.6 million acres.

USDA has already forecast expected declines in production from the record-setting 2025, with Argentina, Australia, and Canada expected to fall annually by 25%, 19%, and 10%, respectively.

In other words: **Canada will have about 10% less wheat than the record 2025**, but this is still a historically strong harvest, because the previous season was exceptional. Australia, Canada, and Kazakhstan all have smaller crops but still historically large crops.

### The European Picture — the Unwitting Third Player

This is the missing piece that ties everything together. **The EU is heading toward a smaller harvest too**, even though 2025/26 was a record year:

EU wheat production for 2026/27 is forecast at 136.8 million tonnes, versus 145.1 million in 2025/26 — a drop of about 6%. Yields are projected to fall to 5.78 tonnes per hectare from 6.06. Total EU grain production (wheat, barley, and corn) is anticipated to revert to average levels at about 277 million tonnes in 2026/27, down from 288.8 million in 2025/26.

The drivers are **the same as in North America**: input costs, especially for fertilizer and fuel, have risen sharply across the EU. The introduction of the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) since January 2026 is making imported fertilizer more expensive, and the ongoing conflict in the Middle East, including the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, has disrupted energy and fertilizer markets.

Additionally: increased frequency of extreme weather events — droughts in Southern and Central Europe, and excessive rainfall during harvest periods in the West — is elevating yield uncertainty and quality variability.

**The crucial link with Canada**: EU wheat imports are expected to rise slightly to 6.5 million tonnes versus 6.2 million in 2025/26. Much of the EU's imports come from Canada, Moldova, and Ukraine, while those from Russia have dropped since 2023/24.

### The Global Picture

Global wheat production is forecast at 819.1 million tonnes, down from last year's record 843.8 million, with the largest reductions for the United States, the EU, Argentina, and Australia. Global corn ending stocks are projected at 277.5 million tonnes, the lowest since 2013/14.

## 5. The Geopolitical Consequence — Where These Two Stories Meet

Now comes the most important conclusion that most analysts miss: **the U.S.-Canada trade war is colliding with the global food shock from the Iran war — and Europe is the unwitting buyer caught between both.**

**What this means in practice:**

1. **Canadian wheat becomes strategically more valuable** — global production is falling, the U.S. harvest is collapsing, Australia is down too. Canada with a harvest of ~32 million tonnes (even with a 10% decline) becomes a key supplier to the Middle East, Japan, Southeast Asia, North Africa **and Europe** — markets that were previously fed by the now-closed Arab and Iran-India routes.

2. **The European weak spot**: EU mills will need imported Canadian wheat for blending and quality reasons. With Russia largely sanctioned out of European markets and Ukrainian volumes constrained by the war and Bosphorus bottlenecks (see the previous Bosphorus analysis), Canada is suddenly Europe's most reliable major supplier. **This gives Carney leverage that Brussels cannot ignore.**

3. **Canadian potash and fertilizer become doubly strategic** — Canada is the world's largest producer of potash. When Arab nitrogen fertilizers are blocked by Hormuz, Canadian potash is the only major available alternative for Western markets. USMCA-compliant goods are exempt from the 10% Section 122 global tariffs, as are energy products and fertilizers — Trump was forced to grant this exemption precisely because the alternative would be a collapse in U.S. agriculture. Europe is now reaching for the same Canadian potash, in direct competition with American farmers.

4. **Canada has its leverage right now** — energy, minerals, potash, wheat. Carney prefers not to call it "leverage," but the fact is that in a time of global shortage, every exporter of these commodities has bargaining power. That's why Carney is in no hurry to strike a USMCA deal before 1 July — time is working in his favor.

5. **The U.S. has its own problem** — its own farmers face sharply increased costs (fertilizers +30-40%, diesel up, tariffs on Canadian potash and fertilizer which have since been reduced but were raised). With its own production declining, the U.S. will need imports — and this puts American politicians in the awkward position of **needing to think very carefully about how not to antagonize Canada right now**.

6. **The European pivot**: Carney's diversification strategy has its natural partner — Brussels. Every Canadian official negotiating with the EU now has a far stronger position than they did a year ago. Canada is moving toward Europe **precisely because Europe needs Canada**. This is a tectonic shift in transatlantic agricultural trade that will outlive both Trump and Carney.

## 6. Forecast for the Next 6 Months

**On trade policy:**
- The USMCA review on 1 July 2026 **will most likely not produce a complete agreement**. Possible scenarios: (a) serial annual reviews, (b) a partial U.S.-Mexico bilateral deal without Canada, (c) extension of discussions.
- Canada continues its diversification, especially with the EU and the Asia-Pacific region.
- Section 232 tariffs (steel, aluminum, autos) **remain in force** — they were not affected by the Supreme Court ruling.

**On grain:**
- Wheat prices in Chicago and Kansas City already surged sharply after the WASDE report on 12 May. July wheat in Kansas City and Chicago finished at the daily limit, up 45 cents, while MIAX Minneapolis closed up 37.5 cents.
- Continued upward pressure is expected through September-October 2026, when the real Canadian, EU, and Russian harvests will become clear.
- **Canada's position on the world market is strengthening** — at precisely the moment when Carney is negotiating with Trump and concluding agreements with Europe.

**On Europe:**
- EU mills and feed industries face a structurally tighter market. Even if intra-EU flows from France, Romania, and Germany hold up, the marginal tonne for blending will come from Canada — at a premium.
- Bread and feed prices in Europe will rise through autumn 2026, especially in countries that depend on imports (the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, Italy, Spain). This will add another layer of inflation on top of the energy and fertilizer shock from Iran.

## 7. Conclusion — The Irony of the Moment

The paradox of the moment: **Trump wants to punish Canada with tariffs, but right now he needs Canadian wheat, potash, crude oil, and aluminum more than ever**. And vice versa: **Carney wants to move away from the United States, but geography won't allow him to do so quickly**. **Europe wants to remain neutral, but it needs Canadian wheat and potash to feed itself.**

The three blocs are moving toward mutual economic separation in the longer run — Canada toward Europe and Asia, the United States toward "fortress," Europe toward selective diversification (Canada, Brazil, Argentina) and away from both Middle Eastern and Russian dependencies — but in the short term (the next 12-18 months) they are more interdependent than ever, because the global food and energy system is under stress.

**Carney is playing the long game.** He knows that Trump's moves can be overturned by the Supreme Court (as IEEPA was), by a change in administration, or by market pressure. That is why he isn't yielding. **Trump is playing the short game** — pushing for quick concessions before 1 July.

**Europe is playing a survival game** — buying what it can, from whom it can, at whatever price. And the most reliable seller standing is Canada.

Who will win? It depends on whether Trump can sustain the political pressure before American inflation (driven by fertilizer and grain) starts to punish him. By late summer 2026, we will know the answer.
